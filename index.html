Long-term Transformation of Sea Surface Temperature, Salinity, and Chlorophyll-a in the Bay of Bengal under Climate Change

Remote Sensing Climate Change Bay of Bengal ORAS5 MODIS/Sentinel
The Bay of Bengal (BoB) stands out as highly vulnerable to climate change due to its geographical location, dynamic regional climate variability, and oceanographic processes. This study aims to examine the long-term (2003–2025) changes of Sea Surface Temperature (SST), Sea Surface Salinity (SSS), and Chlorophyll-a (Chl-a) in the Bay of Bengal through a comprehensive analysis of the high-resolution ECMWF Ocean Reanalysis System 5 (ORAS5) dataset and multi-satellite observations. A significant increasing trend in SST at a rate of 0.0163 °C per year was observed, while SSS showed a gradual decline of −0.0098 PSU per year throughout the years of the study period. The decline in salinity was especially noticeable near the Bangladesh coast because of freshwater inflows from the Ganges–Brahmaputra–Meghna (GBM) river system. Interannual variation in chlorophyll-a (Chl-a) concentration was observed to be significant and associated with the El Niño-Southern Oscillation (ENSO). High Chl-a concentrations were observed during La Niña years (2008 & 2011), whereas low Chl-a concentrations occurred during strong El Niño years (2010 & 2016), except 2024, which was very high in an El Niño condition. The long-term analysis of Chl-a showed a notable decline in surface productivity at a rate of −0.0268 mg m⁻³ per year until 2023, followed by a positive trend between 2024 and 2025. Our findings emphasize a recent shift in productivity and contribute to better understanding of the influence of physical oceanographic processes on biological variability in the Bay of Bengal.

Seasonal and Spatial Variability of Physicochemical Parameters and Heavy Metals in Coastal Surface Water of Bangladesh

Water Quality Heavy Metals ICP-MS MSc Thesis Field Work
Surface water samples were collected from thirty-nine sampling locations along the coast of Bangladesh during pre-monsoon and post-monsoon periods. Five physicochemical parameters (Temperature, pH, DO, Salinity, Turbidity) were measured directly in-situ and ten heavy metals (As, Zn, Pb, Cd, Ni, Mn, Fe, Cr, Cu, Ag) were analyzed using Inductively Coupled Plasma-mass Spectrometry (ICP-MS) to determine the water quality in terms of the Heavy Metal Pollution Index (HPI). The ranges for the physicochemical parameters were 26–30 °C for temperature, 5–33 ppt for salinity, 3–9 mg/L for DO, 6.5–8.8 for pH, and 5–340 NTU for turbidity, while the metals (mg/L) ranged: 0.0002–0.0024 for As, 0.003–0.457 for Zn, 0.0002–0.0085 for Pb, 0.0001–0.0083 for Cd, 0.0004–0.0094 for Ni, 0.003–0.085 for Mn, 0.019–4.56 for Fe, 0.0001–0.0374 for Cr, 0.0003–0.78 for Cu, and 0.0001–4.147 for Ag. Multivariate statistical analysis suggested that high concentrations of heavy metals primarily originated from diverse human activities, while HPI indicated that approximately 80% of the sampling stations exhibited moderate to extreme contamination levels, causing seawater to be unsuitable for mariculture, aquatic ecosystem protection, and irrigation.

Microplastics Pollution in Nijhum Dwip Marine Protected Area and Vicinity: A Case Study from Bangladesh

Microplastics ATR-FTIR MPA Ecological Risk Marine Pollution
Globally, microplastic (MP) pollution is a growing threat to coastal marine environments. We investigated MPs pollution of water and sediment within and adjacent to the Nijhum Dwip Marine Protected Area (MPA) of Bangladesh. A wooden quadrat (50 cm × 50 cm) and a 200-μm plankton net were used for sample collection. For extracting and identifying MPs, microscopes and ATR-FTIR were used for shape, type, color, size, and polymer type. The mean abundance of water and sediment MPs was 30.34 ± 5.92 items/200 gm and 107 ± 19.41 items/m³, respectively. Filament-shaped and fibre-type MPs dominated both sediment (70.88%) and water (74.96%). Sizes ranging from 0.3 to 1 mm and white/transparent color MPs scored the highest proportions. Polyethylene (PE) was the most abundant polymer in water (28.99%), and nylon was the most abundant in sediment (28.18%). Ecological risk assessment (CFI, PLI, NPI, PHI, Er, and RI) indicated moderate to high levels of pollution and varying degrees of risks.

Geospatial Analysis of Shoreline Dynamics of Major Islands in the Meghna River Estuary, Bangladesh

ArcGIS DSAS Shoreline Change Remote Sensing Coastal Management
The analysis of shoreline changes in Manpura, Nijhum Dwip, and Hatiya from 1991 to 2024 highlights distinct trends of erosion and accretion across these islands. Manpura experiences the highest erosion, with 88.31% of its transects showing a mean shoreline change of −11.5 m. In contrast, Nijhum Dwip demonstrates considerable accretion, with 65.98% of transects showing a mean gain of 14.77 m. While Hatiya is mostly affected by erosion, it also shows extensive accretion in the southeastern part of the region. The rate of shoreline changes was determined using DSAS version 5.0, an extension of ArcGIS 10.7 that establishes a baseline, creates orthogonal transects at user-specified intervals along the coast, and calculates rates of change (endpoint rate, linear regression, etc.). The outcome of this analysis might be useful for coastal management and development protection of these islands.
